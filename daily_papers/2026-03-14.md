# Daily Paper Digest — 2026-03-14

## Theme
Abstractions that actually pay rent: recent papers where structure is used to improve planning, sample efficiency, or compositional generalization rather than just decorate the system description.

## Short overview
Today’s strongest paper is **VisualPredicator: Learning Abstract World Models with Neuro-Symbolic Predicates for Robot Planning**. It is the most directly useful because it focuses on the hard part of structured robotics: inventing task-relevant predicates online and using them inside an abstract world model instead of treating symbols as hand-written scaffolding. The broader pattern is that the better recent work is getting more explicit about where abstractions help and where they break: RISE uses a split world-model design for imagined robot improvement, and the object-centric paper is valuable precisely because it tests the representation claim carefully instead of assuming it.

## Ranked papers

1. **VisualPredicator: Learning Abstract World Models with Neuro-Symbolic Predicates for Robot Planning**  
   - Verdict: **Highly relevant**  
   - Role: **Directly relevant**  
   - Why it matters: It tries to learn the symbolic interface itself and then use that interface for abstract planning with OOD evaluation, which is much more methodologically meaningful than simply adding language labels or planner wrappers.

2. **RISE: Self-Improving Robot Policy with Compositional World Model**  
   - Verdict: **Useful**  
   - Role: **Direct utility + methodological inspiration**  
   - Why it matters: The interesting design choice is not “robot RL with imagination” by itself, but the explicit separation between controllable dynamics prediction and progress-value estimation inside the world model.

3. **Are Object-Centric Representations Better At Compositional Generalization?**  
   - Verdict: **Useful**  
   - Role: **Conceptual calibration / evaluation lens**  
   - Why it matters: It is not a new generative or planning model, but it directly tests an assumption that underlies a lot of structured world-model and representation-learning work.

## Most relevant to current work
**VisualPredicator** is the most relevant. It sits directly at the interface of structured representation learning, abstract world modeling, planning, and interpretable neurosymbolic mechanisms.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact
- **Framing impact:** Moderate. These papers support a more disciplined framing: abstractions matter when they improve search, robustness, or data efficiency under constrained resources, not when they merely make the architecture diagram look modular.
- **Related-work impact:** Useful for abstract world models, learned predicates, imagined robot improvement, and object-centric compositional generalization.
- **Novelty threat:** Low to moderate. The closest overlap risk is for any claim about learned symbolic abstractions or compositional world models for planning.
- **Weak-paper warning:** I skipped several recent “agentic” and “multi-agent reasoning” papers in generation because they looked mostly like inference-time orchestration or branding from the abstract alone.

## One-paragraph takeaway
The strongest papers today are the ones that force structure to earn its keep. VisualPredicator is the clearest example: the claim is not just that symbolic abstractions are interpretable, but that learned neuro-symbolic predicates improve sample complexity and OOD generalization in planning. RISE is less interpretable but still methodologically interesting because its compositional world model separates future prediction from progress evaluation, which is a better decomposition than one giant latent video model asked to do everything. The object-centric study is valuable as a corrective: it argues that structured representations help most when data, diversity, or downstream compute are limited, which is a much more believable and useful claim than “object-centric is always better.”
