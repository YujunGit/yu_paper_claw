## Most relevant to current work
**Lyra 2.0** is the most relevant paper today. The transferable lesson is that persistent world generation may benefit from using explicit geometry **as an information-routing interface** rather than forcing rendered geometry or raw frame history to carry the whole burden.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact
- **Method design:** Lyra 2.0 is a strong citation for decoupling geometric tracking from pixel synthesis instead of feeding back rendered 3D memory directly.
- **Representation / interface:** ShapeGen is a useful example of functional structure emerging from geometry-aligned correspondence rather than manual symbolic annotation.
- **Physics framing:** The port-Hamiltonian paper is a good reference if you want to argue that physically structured latent models should preserve meaningful equilibria, not just satisfy a generic energy prior.
- **Citation only:** The planning-domain paper is better as a pointer on symbolic feedback loops than as a model to build on directly.
- **Novelty threat:** Low. Lyra 2.0 overlaps with persistent geometry-aware generative world building, but mainly at the memory/interface level rather than with a broader compositional or neurosymbolic agenda.

## One-paragraph takeaway
The recurring lesson today is that explicit structure matters most when it changes how information flows. Lyra 2.0 uses geometry as a retrieval and correspondence scaffold, which is stronger than just storing more frames or rendering a brittle global memory back into the model. ShapeGen does something similar in robotics: function transfer is grounded in dense shape correspondence, so new examples preserve affordance rather than only appearance. The port-Hamiltonian paper makes the same point in dynamical systems form, where preserving real equilibria is the difference between a physically structured model and a physics-themed approximation.
