# Compositional Planning with Jumpy World Models

## Basic info

* Title: Compositional Planning with Jumpy World Models
* Authors: Jesse Farebrother and collaborators
* Year: 2026
* Venue / source: arXiv
* Link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.19634
* Date surfaced: 2026-04-17
* Why selected in one sentence: It makes temporal abstraction concrete by learning predictive models over the occupancies induced by sequences of pretrained policies across multiple timescales.

## Quick verdict

**Highly relevant**

This is one of the cleaner recent papers on planning with reusable temporally extended actions rather than primitive controls. The key strength is that the world model is matched to the planning object, policy composition over time, instead of being a generic next-step predictor asked to support a different downstream interface. The main caveat is that the setting depends on pretrained policy libraries, so transfer to messier open-ended robotics is not automatic.

## One-paragraph overview

The paper studies long-horizon planning where an agent composes pretrained policies as temporally extended actions. Instead of modeling one-step dynamics at the primitive action level, it learns jumpy world models that predict the state occupancy induced by executing policies over multiple timescales in an off-policy way. It builds on temporal-difference-flow style modeling and adds a consistency objective that aligns predictions across different timescales, then uses those generative predictions to estimate the value of arbitrary policy sequences. The result is a planning setup that reasons directly over reusable action chunks and claims large zero-shot improvements on manipulation and navigation.

## Key questions this summary must address

### 1. What problem is the paper trying to solve?
It is trying to make long-horizon planning with temporally extended actions actually work, instead of falling apart because multi-step policy composition is hard to model accurately from primitive-transition predictors.

### 2. What is the method?
The method learns multi-timescale jumpy world models for pretrained policies, then combines those occupancy-style predictions to estimate the value of executing arbitrary policy sequences. A new cross-timescale consistency loss is used to improve long-horizon predictive alignment.

### 3. What is the method motivation?
If planning is done over policy chunks, the predictive model should also live at that level. Otherwise the planner has to recover temporally abstract reasoning from a predictor trained at the wrong granularity, which makes compounding error and visitation mismatch worse.

### 4. What data does it use?
From the abstract, the model is learned off-policy from data generated by pretrained policies and evaluated on challenging manipulation and navigation tasks. I did not verify the exact environments or data budgets beyond the abstract page.

### 5. How is it evaluated?
It is evaluated by zero-shot planning performance over a range of base policies and tasks, especially longer-horizon settings where primitive-action planning and standard hierarchical baselines struggle.

### 6. What are the main results?
The headline claim is roughly a 200% relative improvement over primitive-action planning on long-horizon tasks, averaged across tested settings. The abstract does not expose the detailed breakdown, so that number still deserves a full-paper check.

### 7. What is actually novel?
The novelty is not just using options or skills. The interesting part is learning occupancy-style jumpy predictive models over policy compositions across multiple timescales, then enforcing cross-timescale consistency so the planner can reason coherently about long-horizon sequences.

### 8. What are the strengths?
- Good match between the predictive interface and the planning interface.
- Temporal abstraction is operational, not just a high-level slogan.
- Multi-timescale consistency is a sensible mechanism for reducing long-horizon drift.
- Transferable framing for any setting with reusable policy fragments or action chunks.

### 9. What are the weaknesses, limitations, or red flags?
- The method inherits whatever biases and blind spots exist in the pretrained policy set.
- Compositionality may be limited if the library lacks crucial coverage.
- Zero-shot gains can look strong while hiding a large upfront cost in learning the constituent policies.
- The world model predicts occupancy behavior induced by policies, which is useful for planning but less obviously reusable for richer counterfactual reasoning.

### 10. What challenges or open problems remain?
Key open problems include learning the policy library jointly, handling uncertainty over policy switching, extending the method to learned latent states or object-centric representations, and dealing with partial observability.

### 11. What future work naturally follows?
- Learn state-dependent policy-switching distributions instead of fixed composition rules.
- Combine jumpy predictive models with object-centric or geometric latent states.
- Compare against action-chunk transformers or hierarchical MPC under matched compute.
- Study whether the same formulation helps in real robot data with imperfect pretrained skills.

### 12. Why does this matter for my work?
It matters because it is a strong example of choosing the right temporal unit of abstraction. If the downstream problem is policy composition, then the representation and dynamics model should probably be built around policy composition too.

### 13. What ideas are steal-worthy?
- Match the predictive model granularity to the planner’s temporal abstraction.
- Learn occupancy-style multi-step predictions rather than only one-step transitions.
- Enforce consistency across timescales as a structural regularizer.
- Treat reusable policy fragments as compositional planning primitives rather than mere action accelerators.

### 14. Final decision
**Read.** This is a serious planning paper with a mechanism that could transfer beyond the exact benchmarks if the abstraction assumption is appropriate.
